## Abstract

This repository proposes a conceptual and institutional transition from offset-based carbon credits to physically measurable **Cooling Credits**.

Carbon credits have developed as a system for evaluating CO₂ emission reductions, removals, avoidance, or absorption through accounting mechanisms. However, on a global scale, atmospheric CO₂ concentration continues to rise, and global average temperature has not decreased. At least in its current offset-based form, the carbon credit system has not functioned as a mechanism for physically cooling the Earth.

Cooling Credits are different.

A Cooling Credit is not a book-based offset for CO₂ emissions. It is a credit assigned to actions that measurably reduce heat load in regions, cities, soils, forests, oceans, agricultural lands, buildings, and living environments.

The central proposition of this repository is simple:

```text
The Earth cannot be cooled by accounting.
The Earth cools only when heat is physically reduced.
```

## 1. Background: Why Carbon Credits Are Not Enough

Modern climate policy has long been designed around CO₂ emission reduction.

This is important. Greenhouse gas reduction, decarbonization, renewable energy, energy efficiency, and carbon sequestration will remain essential.

However, climate damage ultimately appears as **heat**.

Extreme heat, urban heat islands, droughts, wildfires, rising sea surface temperatures, coral bleaching, marine heatwaves, crop damage, rising cooling demand, and accumulated urban waste heat are not merely issues of atmospheric CO₂ concentration.

They are issues of heat accumulated within the Earth system, urban structures, soils, forests, oceans, and broken water cycles.

Carbon credits mainly deal with emission accounting and carbon balance.

But at the actual field level, the questions are different.

* Has local air temperature actually decreased?
* Has surface temperature decreased?
* Has WBGT improved?
* Has soil regained the ability to retain water?
* Has forest evapotranspiration recovered?
* Has ocean surface heat risk been reduced?
* Has urban waste heat been lowered?
* Has the ecosystem's natural cooling function recovered?

Cooling Credits are proposed as a framework for answering these physical questions.

## 2. Structural Problem of Offset-Based Carbon Credits

The basic structure of offset-based carbon credits can be summarized as follows:

```text
A company or organization emits CO₂
↓
A reduction, removal, absorption, or conservation action is credited elsewhere
↓
The emission is offset in accounting terms
↓
The company can claim net-zero, carbon neutrality, or climate action
```

At first glance, this appears rational.

However, the structural problem is that the system can encourage the justification of continued emissions rather than direct reduction of actual emissions.

Offset-based carbon credits have several recurring risks:

* A company may be treated as having acted on climate change without directly reducing its own emissions.
* It can be difficult to verify whether forest conservation, afforestation, renewable energy, or carbon-removal projects are truly additional.
* The claimed effect may not be permanent due to forest fires, land-use change, abandonment, or future degradation.
* Double counting, overestimation, and arbitrary baseline design can occur.
* Credit markets can become financialized, shifting focus from field-level physical improvement to tradable value.
* Ordinary citizens often cannot feel what has actually improved.

In this sense, offset-based carbon credits can become an accounting process that appears to be climate action.

They are not necessarily a system that cools the planet.

## 3. Carbon Credits as a License to Emit

Environmental systems should exist to reduce environmental damage.

However, offset-based carbon credits can structurally drift toward the following logic:

```text
Not a system for reducing emissions,
but a system for purchasing a reason to keep emitting.
```

If buying credits is cheaper than upgrading facilities, redesigning production processes, or changing energy systems, the incentive for actual emission reduction becomes weaker.

If credit markets become profitable for investors, they may function more as financial products than as climate solutions.

At that point, accounting alignment becomes more important than whether the Earth is physically cooling.

This is the greatest danger of carbon credits.

The Earth cannot be cooled by accounting.

## 4. Climate Risk Is Already Appearing as Heat

The climate crisis will not suddenly begin in the future.

It is already appearing as heat.

Examples include:

* Increase in extreme heat days
* Urban heat islands
* Rising wildfire risk
* Drought
* Rising sea surface temperatures
* Marine heatwaves
* Coral bleaching
* Heat stress in crops
* Heatstroke risk
* Increased cooling demand
* Instability in ecological networks

The important point is that ecological collapse is not occurring only in visible places.

Coral bleaching is a relatively visible phenomenon. However, deeper changes may already be occurring in soil microbes, fungi, plankton, insects, plant root zones, symbiotic relationships, parasitic relationships, and food webs.

Humanity understands only a small part of the biological networks that sustain ecosystems.

When one species disappears, we may not fully understand which organisms depended on it as food, which plants depended on it for pollination, which microbial cycles it influenced, or which ecological balance it helped maintain.

Therefore, the loss of a single species may not be only a local extinction.

It may become a trigger for cascading collapse within an ecological network.

In this sense, climate action must include not only CO₂ accounting, but also the regeneration of ecosystems and natural cooling functions.

## 5. Keystone Species and Invisible Ecological Networks

Ecosystems contain organisms known as keystone species.

A keystone species may not be the most numerous species, but it plays a disproportionate role in maintaining the structure of an ecosystem.

When a keystone species disappears, food webs, vegetation structures, predator-prey relationships, symbiotic relationships, and soil cycles can collapse in a chain reaction.

However, humanity has identified only a limited number of keystone species.

Unknown microbes, fungi, ocean plankton, soil organisms, insects, parasites, and symbiotic organisms may also play keystone-like roles that are not yet understood.

Therefore, ecosystem loss caused by rising temperature is not simply a reduction in biodiversity.

It is also the collapse of networks that support the Earth's natural cooling system.

## 7. Definition of Cooling Credit

A **Cooling Credit** is a credit assigned to an activity that produces measurable or estimable heat-load reduction in regions, cities, soils, forests, oceans, agricultural lands, buildings, living environments, or industrial systems.

More simply:

```text
A Cooling Credit is a system that assigns value to actions that physically reduce heat.
```

The evaluation target is not a book-based offset of CO₂ emissions.

The evaluation target is actual cooling contribution.

Possible evaluation indicators include:

* Air-temperature reduction
* Surface-temperature reduction
* WBGT improvement
* Soil water-retention recovery
* Plant evapotranspiration recovery
* Forest regeneration
* Humus production
* Reduction of organic-waste incineration
* Urban waste-heat reduction
* Water-cycle recovery
* Ocean-circulation recovery
* Potential fishery recovery
* Desert greening
* Tourism-resource recovery
* Regional heat-stress reduction

A Cooling Credit is not a license to emit.

A Cooling Credit is proof of cooling contribution.

## 8. Difference Between Carbon Credits and Cooling Credits

Carbon credits and Cooling Credits may appear similar, but their foundations are fundamentally different.

The core metric of carbon credits is CO₂ emissions.

The core metric of Cooling Credits is heat-load reduction.

The essence of carbon credits is book-based offsetting.

The essence of Cooling Credits is physical cooling.

Carbon credits give companies a path to offset emissions.

Cooling Credits give companies a path to invest in businesses that reduce heat.

The results of carbon credits are often difficult for ordinary people to see or feel.

The results of Cooling Credits can be measured and experienced through air temperature, surface temperature, WBGT, soil moisture, vegetation recovery, water-cycle recovery, and local cooling effects.

Carbon credits mainly evaluate emission reduction, absorption, conservation, or offsetting.

Cooling Credits evaluate air-temperature reduction, surface-temperature reduction, WBGT improvement, water-cycle restoration, soil water retention, forest regeneration, urban cooling, and ocean-circulation recovery.

The risk of carbon credits is that they can become a license to emit.

The challenge of Cooling Credits is that they require measurement, MRV, and correction for local conditions.

Carbon credits affect the Earth indirectly and often invisibly.

Cooling Credits can potentially produce direct physical heat-load reduction.

This difference is decisive for institutional design.

Cooling Credit is not merely an environmental philosophy.

It is an institutional mechanism for replacing the engine of nature destruction with the engine of nature regeneration.

* Shade design
* Outdoor air-conditioner heat countermeasures
* Waterway and waterfront restoration
* Urban waste-heat reduction
* Heat countermeasures for parks, schools, and shopping streets

Possible indicators include air temperature, surface temperature, WBGT, water use, electricity use, cooled area, and operating time.

### 10.2 Soil Regeneration

Soil regeneration may include:

* Humus production
* Organic-waste circulation
* Food-waste composting
* Soil water-retention recovery
* Microbial-environment recovery
* Reduced dependence on chemical agriculture
* Lower surface temperature in farmland
* Plant evapotranspiration recovery

Possible indicators include soil moisture, soil organic matter, surface temperature, vegetation cover, and crop stability.

### 10.3 Forest Regeneration

Forest regeneration may include:

* Abandoned cedar forest regeneration
* Conversion from monoculture forests to mixed forests
* Native forest recovery
* Fruit forest conversion
* Edible wild plant and mushroom forest systems
* Watershed recharge
* Wildfire-risk reduction
* Humus-layer recovery

Possible indicators include surface temperature, soil moisture, vegetation cover, evapotranspiration, multi-layer forest structure, and watershed recharge.

## 12. MRV: Monitoring, Reporting, and Verification

MRV is essential for institutionalizing Cooling Credits.

MRV stands for:

* Monitoring
* Reporting
* Verification

Monitoring may include:

* Air temperature
* Surface temperature
* WBGT
* Humidity
* Soil moisture
* Vegetation cover
* Water use
* Electricity use
* Incineration reduction
* Humus production amount
* Forest regeneration area
* Ocean observation data

Reporting may include:

* Actions implemented
* Area covered
* Duration
* Measurement method
* Sensor information
* Photographic records
* CSV data
* Cooling Credit Score

Verification may include checking:

* Whether data has been altered
* Whether the measurement method is valid
* Whether a cooling effect is actually observed
* Whether side effects exist
* Whether the method is suitable for the local environment
* Whether the effect can be sustained over time

Where carbon credits often depend on accounting-based offsetting, Cooling Credits depend on physical measurement in the field.

This is both the strength and the responsibility of the system.
