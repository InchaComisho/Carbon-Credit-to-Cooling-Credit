## Overview

This page provides an English visual summary of the core argument of this repository:

> carbon-pricing and carbon-credit-related business can grow while atmospheric CO₂ concentration and global annual CO₂ emissions do not physically decline.

The chart is not intended to claim that every carbon-pricing mechanism has no effect in every region. Rather, it shows that at the global physical level, atmospheric CO₂ concentration and annual emissions have not been reduced in proportion to the growth of carbon-pricing revenue and credit-related business activity.

## What the Graph Shows

### 1. Atmospheric CO₂ Concentration

The atmospheric CO₂ concentration measured at Mauna Loa has continued to rise from the late 1950s to the present.

### 2. Global Annual CO₂ Emissions

Global annual CO₂ emissions have increased strongly since the second half of the 20th century. Even after the temporary decline during the COVID-19 pandemic, emissions rebounded and have not shown a sustained global decline.

### 3. Global Carbon Pricing Revenue

Carbon-pricing revenue and carbon-market-related business activity have expanded significantly in recent years.

### 4. Indexed Comparison

When carbon-pricing revenue and global CO₂ emissions are indexed to 2017 = 100, carbon-pricing revenue rises sharply, while global CO₂ emissions remain roughly flat or slightly higher.

This supports the central argument:

```text
Carbon accounting can expand without physically cooling the Earth.
Cooling Credits must evaluate actual heat-load reduction.
```

---

## Interpretation

The purpose of this chart is to clarify the difference between **carbon accounting** and **physical cooling**.

Carbon credits and carbon-pricing mechanisms may create markets, revenues, and accounting structures. However, if atmospheric CO₂ concentration and global annual emissions continue to rise, then the system has not yet produced the physical planetary cooling effect required to prevent climate breakdown.

Cooling Credits are proposed as a complementary and corrective framework:

- not merely offsetting emissions on paper,
- but investing in measurable heat-load reduction,
- including urban cooling, soil water retention, forest regeneration, humus production, evapotranspiration recovery, water-cycle recovery, and ocean heat-risk mitigation.

## Sources and Notes

- Atmospheric CO₂ concentration: NOAA Global Monitoring Laboratory, Mauna Loa annual mean CO₂.
- Global annual CO₂ emissions: Our World in Data / Global Carbon Project.
- Global carbon-pricing revenue: World Bank State and Trends of Carbon Pricing reports; simplified annual values for visual comparison.

The carbon-pricing revenue series is used as an illustrative comparison, not as a complete accounting of all carbon-credit markets.
