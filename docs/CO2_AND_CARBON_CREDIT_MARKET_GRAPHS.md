## Overview

This page provides a corrected visual summary of the core argument of this repository.

The previous comparison between carbon-pricing revenue and global CO₂ emissions could be misunderstood if it made emissions appear simply "flat." The more important scientific distinction is this:

```text
Annual CO₂ emissions are a flow.
Atmospheric CO₂ concentration is a stock.
```

Even if annual emissions were flat, atmospheric CO₂ concentration would continue to rise as long as total emissions exceed what land, oceans, and managed carbon-removal actions can absorb.

In addition, natural carbon sinks and natural cooling functions can weaken under heat, drought, wildfire, soil degradation, forest loss, and ocean stress.

## What These Graphs Show

### 1. Atmospheric CO₂ concentration is a stock

Atmospheric CO₂ concentration keeps accumulating because residual emissions remain in the atmosphere after land and ocean sinks absorb only part of annual emissions.

### 2. Global annual CO₂ emissions are still historically high

Some industrialized economies have reduced emissions, but global emissions remain historically high because growth elsewhere offsets part of those reductions. The temporary pandemic decline did not become a sustained global decline.

### 3. Carbon-pricing revenue can grow without cooling the Earth

Carbon-pricing and carbon-credit-related business can expand while global physical indicators continue to move in the wrong direction.

### 4. The key issue is the emissions gap after absorption

The important question is not only how much CO₂ is emitted, but how much remains after natural and managed absorption.

```text
Human emissions
↓
Land and ocean sinks absorb only part
↓
Residual CO₂ remains in the atmosphere
↓
Atmospheric CO₂ concentration rises
```

### 5. Natural sinks are not fixed machines

Forests, soils, wetlands, and oceans do not have a fixed, guaranteed processing capacity. Their strength varies by year and region, and can weaken under climate stress.

This means the climate crisis is not only a problem of emissions. It is also a problem of weakened natural absorption and weakened natural cooling.

## Corrected Interpretation

The stronger and more accurate argument is not:

```text
Carbon revenue increased, but emissions stayed flat.
```

The correct argument is:

```text
Carbon revenue can increase while global emissions remain historically high.
Because emissions are a flow and atmospheric CO₂ is a stock,
CO₂ concentration continues to rise whenever residual emissions exceed absorption.
At the same time, natural carbon sinks and natural cooling functions are under stress.
```

This is why Cooling Credits are needed.

Cooling Credits should not only evaluate carbon accounting. They should evaluate measurable heat-load reduction and restoration of the systems that buffer heat and carbon:

- soil water retention,
- humus production,
- forest regeneration,
- evapotranspiration recovery,
- water-cycle recovery,
- urban cooling,
- organic-waste circulation,
- ocean observation and heat-risk mitigation.

---

## Caution

This page does not claim that land and ocean sinks have permanently collapsed at the global level.

Land and ocean systems still absorb a large share of human CO₂ emissions on average. However, their strength can weaken under drought, wildfire, heat, land degradation, and ocean stress. In some regions and years, natural sinks can weaken sharply or temporarily shift toward net emissions.

Therefore, the safe conclusion is:

```text
The Earth system may already be operating beyond its stable carbon-processing and heat-buffering capacity under current human pressure.
```
